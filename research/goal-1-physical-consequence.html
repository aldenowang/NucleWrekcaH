Research Goal 01

Grounding Model Failure in Physical Consequence

A "successful attack" against a surrogate model is currently defined in purely statistical terms — a spike in prediction error relative to the ground-truth solver. That definition is useful for benchmarking, but it says nothing about whether the error actually matters. This goal reclassifies surrogate-model failure in terms of real-world consequence: instead of asking "how large is the error," we ask "would this error, if it occurred in a real coolant channel, push the system past a limit that operators actually care about."

Failure Criteria

We define a physical failure event as either of the following being violated:

Pressure Limit
$P_{\max} \approx 9.39\ \text{MPa}$

Maximum allowable pipe-wall pressure, derived from ASME pipe parameters via Barlow's formula.

Temperature Limit
$T_{\text{wall}}(z) < 800^{\circ}\text{C}$

Maximum service temperature of 316H stainless steel, at any axial position z.

Deriving the Wall Temperature

The wall temperature at axial position z follows from Newton's law of cooling, relating it to the local fluid temperature and the local heat flux through the convective coefficient h:

$$T_{\text{wall}}(z) = T_{\text{fluid}}(z) + \dfrac{q''(z)}{h(z)}$$

The heat flux itself follows the cosine-shaped axial power profile used in Roy et al. (2026):

$$q''(z) = q_{\max}\,\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi z}{H}\right)$$

The convective coefficient h is not constant — it depends on the flow regime and the coolant's thermal properties. We compute it with the Dittus–Boelter correlation, the standard closure for turbulent forced convection in a pipe:

$$h = 0.023\left(\dfrac{\rho\, v\, D_h}{\mu}\right)^{0.8}\left(\dfrac{c_p\, \mu}{k}\right)^{0.4}\cdot\dfrac{k}{D_h}$$

The first bracketed term is the Reynolds number (inertial vs. viscous forces), raised to the 0.8 power; the second is the Prandtl number (momentum vs. thermal diffusivity), raised to the 0.4 power — together the Nusselt correlation $\text{Nu} = 0.023\,\text{Re}^{0.8}\,\text{Pr}^{0.4}$, converted to a dimensional coefficient via $k/D_h$.

With q″ and h in hand, Tfluid(z) follows from a conservation-of-energy balance on the coolant: as fluid moves up the channel, it absorbs heat through the wetted perimeter Pw. Integrating that heat gain from the inlet to z and substituting into Newton's law of cooling above gives the full wall-temperature profile:

$$T_{\text{wall}}(z) = T_{\text{in}} + \dfrac{P_w\, q_{\max}\displaystyle\int_0^{z}\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi z'}{H}\right)dz'}{\rho\, v\, A_{\text{cross}}} \;+\; \dfrac{q_{\max}\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi z}{H}\right)}{0.023\left(\dfrac{\rho\, v\, D_h}{\mu}\right)^{0.8}\left(\dfrac{c_p\, \mu}{k}\right)^{0.4}\cdot\dfrac{k}{D_h}}$$

The integral evaluates in closed form to $\dfrac{H}{\pi}\left[1-\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi z}{H}\right)\right]$. Substituting that in — and keeping h fully expanded — gives the final closed-form profile:

$$T_{\text{wall}}(z) = T_{\text{in}} + \dfrac{P_w\, q_{\max}\, H}{\pi\,\rho\, v\, A_{\text{cross}}}\left[1-\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi z}{H}\right)\right] \;+\; \dfrac{q_{\max}\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi z}{H}\right)}{0.023\left(\dfrac{\rho\, v\, D_h}{\mu}\right)^{0.8}\left(\dfrac{c_p\, \mu}{k}\right)^{0.4}\cdot\dfrac{k}{D_h}}$$

The first term is the bulk fluid temperature rise from absorbed heat, growing monotonically with z. The second is the extra temperature jump across the wall's boundary layer, tracking the local heat flux shape directly.

Variable definitions: Tin is an input to the surrogate model — the coolant's temperature as it enters the channel. v is the coolant velocity, taken from the model's outputs. Pw is the wetted perimeter, the perimeter of the channel's cross section, and Across is the area of that cross section. H is the length of the pipe, and Dh its hydraulic diameter. ρ is the fluid's density, μ its dynamic viscosity, cp its specific heat capacity, and k its thermal conductivity.

Why This Matters

An adversarial input that merely inflates a surrogate model's mean-squared error is a benchmark curiosity. An adversarial input that, once mapped through the pressure limit and this wall-temperature model, pushes P above Pmax or Twall(z) above 800°C for any z is a safety-relevant finding — it identifies a region of the input space where trusting the surrogate model would mean missing a real physical failure mode.
